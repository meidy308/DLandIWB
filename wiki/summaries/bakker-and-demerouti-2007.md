# Bakker & Demerouti (2007) – The Job Demands-Resources model: state of the art

**Summary**: This paper provides a comprehensive overview of the Job Demands-Resources (JD-R) model, summarizing its core propositions, empirical evidence for dual processes and interaction effects, and extending the framework to include personal resources and job crafting.

**Sources**: Bakker and Demerouti - 2007.md

---

## Bibliographic Details

- **Author**: Arnold B. Bakker (Erasmus University Rotterdam) and Evangelia Demerouti (Utrecht University)
- **Year**: 2007
- **Journal**: Journal of Managerial Psychology, 22(3), 309-328
- **Methodology**: Conceptual Review / Meta-analytic summary of JD-R studies
- **Sample**: Various (Call centre employees, nutrition production workers, teachers, dentists, etc.)
- **Analysis**: Structural Equation Modeling (SEM), moderated regression
- **Data collection**: Multiple studies between 2000-2006

## Hypotheses and Results

| Hypothesis | Description | Result |
|---|---|---|
| Dual Process 1 | Job demands $\rightarrow$ Burnout $\rightarrow$ Health problems / Absenteeism | **Supported** (source: Bakker and Demerouti - 2007.md, p. 313-316) |
| Dual Process 2 | Job resources $\rightarrow$ Work engagement $\rightarrow$ Organizational commitment / Performance | **Supported** (source: Bakker and Demerouti - 2007.md, p. 313-316) |
| Buffer Effect | Job resources buffer the impact of job demands on burnout | **Supported** (source: Bakker and Demerouti - 2007.md, p. 317) |
| Saliency Effect | Job resources gain motivational potential when job demands are high | **Supported** (source: Bakker and Demerouti - 2007.md, p. 318-319) |

## Key Findings

### The Dual Process Model
The paper confirms two distinct psychological processes:
- **Health Impairment Process**: Chronic job demands exhaust mental and physical resources, leading to depletion of energy (exhaustion) and health problems (source: Bakker and Demerouti - 2007.md, p. 313).
- **Motivational Process**: Job resources play an intrinsic motivational role (satisfying needs for autonomy, competence, and relatedness) or an extrinsic motivational role (achieving work goals), leading to engagement (source: Bakker and Demerouti - 2007.md, p. 313-314).

### Interaction Effects
- **Buffering Job Demands**: Various resources (e.g., autonomy, social support, feedback, high-quality supervisor relationship) can protect employees from the negative impact of specific demands like work overload or emotional demands (source: Bakker and Demerouti - 2007.md, p. 317).
- **Boosting Work Engagement**: Resources become most beneficial for engagement when employees are confronted with high demands (e.g., skill variety boosts engagement most when workload is high) (source: Bakker and Demerouti - 2007.md, p. 318).

### Personal Resources and Job Crafting
- **Personal Resources**: Self-efficacy, optimism, and organizational-based self-esteem partially mediate the relationship between job resources and engagement (source: Bakker and Demerouti - 2007.md, p. 323).
- **Job Crafting**: Employees are active crafters who shape and redefine their task and relationship boundaries to align them with their needs and abilities (source: Bakker and Demerouti - 2007.md, p. 322).

## Contradictions

- No significant contradictions noted, but it critiques static models like the Demand-Control Model for being too limited in their selection of variables (source: Bakker and Demerouti - 2007.md, p. 311).

## Practical Implications

- **JD-R Monitor**: An internet application used to provide employees with personalized feedback on their demands and resources compared to benchmarks (source: Bakker and Demerouti - 2007.md, p. 324).
- **Tailor-made Interventions**: Organizations should identify specific risk factors through qualitative interviews before quantitative mapping to design effective interventions (source: Bakker and Demerouti - 2007.md, p. 323).

## Limitation

- **Common Method Variance**: Many early studies relied exclusively on self-reports (source: Bakker and Demerouti - 2007.md, p. 316).
- **Static snapshots**: The need for more longitudinal studies to confirm reciprocal relationships and "loss/gain spirals" (source: Bakker and Demerouti - 2007.md, p. 321).

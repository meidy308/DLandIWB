# Bakker et al. (2003) – Dual processes at work in a call centre: An application of the job demands–resources model

**Summary**: This study examines the predictive validity of the Job Demands-Resources (JD-R) model among call centre employees, demonstrating that job demands primarily predict absenteeism via health problems, whereas job resources predict turnover intentions via involvement.

**Sources**: Bakker et al. - 2003.md

---

## Bibliographic Details

- **Author**: Arnold B. Bakker, Evangelia Demerouti, & Wilmar B. Schaufeli
- **Year**: 2003
- **Journal**: European Journal of Work and Organizational Psychology, 12(4), 393-417
- **Methodology**: Quantitative
- **Sample**: 477 customer service employees working in the call centre of a large Dutch telecom company
- **Analysis**: Structural Equation Modeling (SEM)
- **Data collection**: Electronic and paper-and-pencil questionnaires

## Research Model

### The Health Impairment Process
- **Independent variable**: [[job-demands]] (workload, changes in tasks, emotional demands, computer problems)
- **Mediator**: Health problems ([[exhaustion]] and [[repetitive-strain-injury]])
- **Dependent variable**: [[absenteeism]] (absence duration and long-term absence)

### The Motivational Process
- **Independent variable**: [[job-resources]] (social support, supervisory coaching, performance feedback, time control)
- **Mediator**: Involvement ([[affective-commitment]] and [[dedication]])
- **Dependent variable**: [[turnover-intentions]]

## Hypotheses and Results

| Hypothesis | Description | Result |
|---|---|---|
| H1 (Health Impairment) | Job demands → Health problems (Exhaustion & RSI) → Absenteeism | **Supported** (Job demands → Health problems: β = .60, p < .01; Health problems → Absenteeism: β = .25, p < .01) |
| H2 (Motivational) | Job resources → Involvement (Commitment & Dedication) → Turnover intentions | **Supported** (Job resources → Involvement: β = .52, p < .01; Involvement → Turnover intentions: β = -.77, p < .01) |

*Note: Health problems did not act as a pure mediator but as a conditional variable, whereas involvement acted as a pure mediator.*

## Key Findings

### The Health Impairment Process
Job demands, specifically work overload, changes in tasks, emotional demands, and computer problems, were the most important predictors of exhaustion and repetitive strain injury (RSI). These health problems, in turn, were the only predictors of sickness absenteeism (source: Bakker et al. - 2003.md, p. 409). 

### The Motivational Process
Job resources, specifically social support by colleagues, supervisory coaching, performance feedback, and time control, were unique predictors of organizational commitment and dedication. These feelings of involvement indirectly lowered turnover intentions, acting as a full mediator (source: Bakker et al. - 2003.md, p. 409).

### Cross-Links Between the Processes
Although the primary dual processes were supported, the study found two small cross-links:
- An increase in job resources coincided with a small decrease in health problems (β = -.25, p < .01), suggesting some resources directly prevent energy-depletion (source: Bakker et al. - 2003.md, p. 410).
- Health problems had a small but significant positive relationship with turnover intentions (β = .18, p < .01), suggesting health impairment may directly result in psychological withdrawal over and above the impact of low involvement (source: Bakker et al. - 2003.md, p. 410). 

### Job Position Differences
The study highlighted that different job positions experience different levels of demands and resources. Teleconsultants and supervisors reported high workload and computer problems, while teleconsultants additionally reported the highest scores on exhaustion, RSI, and sickness absence. Conversely, teleoperators reported the lowest dedication and highest turnover intentions (source: Bakker et al. - 2003.md, p. 408-409).

## Practical Implications
To decrease absenteeism, organizations should intervene in the health impairment path by reducing specific job demands (e.g., resolving computer problems, optimizing workload). Conversely, to increase involvement and lower turnover intentions, they must address the motivational path by providing necessary job resources (e.g., social support, coaching, time control) (source: Bakker et al. - 2003.md, p. 412).

## Limitation
The author explicitly recognized limitations regarding cross-sectional data, which precludes concluding causal relationships, and the reliance on self-reported measures for all variables, including absenteeism, which might inflate relationships due to common method variance (source: Bakker et al. - 2003.md, p. 411). However, tests controlling for negative affectivity showed the core JD-R relationships held strong, even making the cross-links non-significant (source: Bakker et al. - 2003.md, p. 412). A limitation not stated by the authors is that generalizing these specific demands and resources to other industries is cautioned, as JD-R is heavily context-dependent, and the study does not capture broader organizational structural elements outside the immediate work environment.

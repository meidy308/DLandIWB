# Bakker & Demerouti (2014) – Job Demands–Resources Theory

**Summary**: This paper formalizes the Job Demands-Resources (JD-R) model into a full-fledged theory, integrating earlier job design and stress models. It details the dual pathways (health impairment and motivation), interaction effects, the role of personal resources, and the dynamic nature of job characteristics through reversed causal effects and job crafting.

**Sources**: Bakker and Demerouti - 2014.md

## Hypotheses and Results

| Hypothesis | Description | Result |
|---|---|---|
| Dual Processes | Demands predict strain; Resources predict engagement. | **Supported** (source: Bakker and Demerouti - 2014.md, p. 9) |
| Buffer Hypothesis | Resources mitigate the impact of demands on strain. | **Supported** (source: Bakker and Demerouti - 2014.md, p. 11) |
| Saliency Hypothesis | Resources boost engagement most when demands are high. | **Supported** (source: Bakker and Demerouti - 2014.md, p. 11) |
| Reversed Causality | Strain increases demands; Engagement increases resources. | **Supported** (source: Bakker and Demerouti - 2014.md, p. 14) |
| Job Crafting | Employees proactively change demands/resources. | **Supported** (source: Bakker and Demerouti - 2014.md, p. 16) |

## Key Findings

### From Model to Theory
The JD-R model matured into a theory after extensive empirical support and meta-analyses (source: Bakker and Demerouti - 2014.md, p. 8). It addresses the limitations of early models (DCM, ERI) by being flexible, considering both stress and motivation, and acknowledging the dynamic nature of work (source: Bakker and Demerouti - 2014.md, p. 5-7).

### The Dual Process Model
The theory posits two fairly independent processes:
1. **[[health-impairment-process]]**: Job demands cost effort and consume energy, leading to exhaustion and ill-health (source: Bakker and Demerouti - 2014.md, p. 9).
2. **[[motivational-process]]**: Job resources fulfill basic psychological needs, leading to work engagement and performance (source: Bakker and Demerouti - 2014.md, p. 9).

### Dynamic Nature and Spirals
JD-R theory recognizes reversed causal effects:
- **Loss Spirals**: Exhausted employees may evaluate demands more critically or perform poorly, creating more job demands over time (source: Bakker and Demerouti - 2014.md, p. 14).
- **Gain Spirals**: Engaged employees mobilize their own resources (e.g., seeking support, feedback), leading to a further increase in resources and engagement (source: Bakker and Demerouti - 2014.md, p. 14-15).

### Job Crafting as a Mechanism
[[job-crafting]] is defined as the proactive changes employees make to their job demands and resources (source: Bakker and Demerouti - 2014.md, p. 16). It serves as the behavior through which employees manage the dynamic relationship between themselves and their environment (source: Bakker and Demerouti - 2014.md, p. 17).

### Intervention Taxonomy
Interventions are classified by level (Individual/Organization) and target (Work Environment/Person) (source: Bakker and Demerouti - 2014.md, p. 18):
- **Job Redesign**: Organization-level, Environment-target.
- **Training**: Organization-level, Person-target (e.g., PsyCap training).
- **Job Crafting**: Individual-level, Environment-target.
- **Strengths-based**: Individual-level, Person-target.

## Contradictions
The paper notes that evidence for earlier models like the Demand-Control Model (DCM) is mixed, particularly regarding interaction effects, which the JD-R theory addresses by allowing for a wider range of resources and demands (source: Bakker and Demerouti - 2014.md, p. 4).

## Practical Implications
Organizations should not only focus on top-down job redesign but also encourage bottom-up job crafting. Interventions should target both the environment (resources) and the person (personal resources/strengths) to optimize engagement and performance (source: Bakker and Demerouti - 2014.md, p. 22).

## Limitation
The authors note that while some longitudinal evidence exists for reversed causality, more research is needed to fully understand how these relationships develop over time (source: Bakker and Demerouti - 2014.md, p. 14). Additionally, strengths-based interventions in the workplace lack extensive scientific evaluation compared to general wellbeing contexts (source: Bakker and Demerouti - 2014.md, p. 21).

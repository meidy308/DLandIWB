# Bakker et al. (2014) – Burnout and Work Engagement: The JD–R Approach

**Summary**: This review provides a comprehensive analysis of burnout and work engagement within the JD-R framework, detailing their antecedents, consequences, and the evolution of JD-R from a model to a mature theory including job crafting and interventions.

**Sources**: Bakker et al. - 2014.md

## Hypotheses and Results

| Hypothesis | Description | Result |
|---|---|---|
| Dual Processes | Demands predict burnout; Resources predict engagement. | **Supported** (source: Bakker et al. - 2014.md, p. 391, 393) |
| Buffer Hypothesis | Job resources mitigate the impact of job demands on burnout. | **Supported** (source: Bakker et al. - 2014.md, p. 392) |
| Saliency Hypothesis | Job resources boost engagement most when demands are high. | **Supported** (source: Bakker et al. - 2014.md, p. 394) |
| Performance Link | Burnout (especially exhaustion) relates to lower performance; Engagement relates to higher performance. | **Supported** (source: Bakker et al. - 2014.md, p. 396, 398) |
| Job Crafting | Job crafting leads to increased engagement and mediates the proactive personality-engagement link. | **Supported** (source: Bakker et al. - 2014.md, p. 395, 401) |

## Key Findings

### Antecedents of Burnout and Engagement
- **Burnout**: Primarily predicted by situational job demands like role ambiguity, role conflict, and workload (source: Bakker et al. - 2014.md, p. 392). Individual factors like low emotional stability and extraversion also contribute (source: Bakker et al. - 2014.md, p. 393).
- **Engagement**: Primarily driven by job resources such as task variety, autonomy, and social support (source: Bakker et al. - 2014.md, p. 393). Conscientiousness and proactive personality are key individual predictors (source: Bakker et al. - 2014.md, p. 395).

### Consequences
- **Burnout**: Leads to severe health problems including depressive disorders, anxiety, cardiovascular diseases, and even increased mortality risk (source: Bakker et al. - 2014.md, p. 395-396). It strongly predicts sickness absence duration (source: Bakker et al. - 2014.md, p. 397).
- **Engagement**: Associated with positive emotions, active learning, and proactive behavior (source: Bakker et al. - 2014.md, p. 397). It predicts both task and contextual (extra-role) performance, such as organizational citizenship behavior (OCB) (source: Bakker et al. - 2014.md, p. 398).

### The Role of Job Crafting
The authors adopt Tims et al. (2012) definition of [[job-crafting]] involving four behaviors: (1) increasing structural resources, (2) increasing social resources, (3) increasing challenges, and (4) decreasing hindrance demands (source: Bakker et al. - 2014.md, p. 401). Job crafting is seen as a key mechanism for maintaining engagement and can be trained (source: Bakker et al. - 2014.md, p. 402).

### Interventions
A taxonomy of interventions is proposed:
1. **Organizational Level**: Optimizing demands (reducing hindrances), increasing resources, and fostering personal resources through training (source: Bakker et al. - 2014.md, p. 402-403).
2. **Individual Level**: Job crafting training, strengths use training, and recovery training (source: Bakker et al. - 2014.md, p. 403).

## Contradictions
The review notes that whereas some studies found personal resources mediate the resource-engagement link, they do not necessarily offset the demand-exhaustion link (source: Bakker et al. - 2014.md, p. 401).

## Practical Implications
Interventions should combine top-down (job redesign) and bottom-up (job crafting) approaches. Monitoring daily fluctuations in engagement can help identify situational triggers for high performance (source: Bakker et al. - 2014.md, p. 403-404).

## Limitation
The literature has largely ignored daily and momentary fluctuations (within-person view) and the link to observable behaviors beyond self-reports (source: Bakker et al. - 2014.md, p. 403-404).

# Scholze and Hecker (2024) – The job demands-resources model as a theoretical lens for the bright and dark side of digitization

**Summary**: This study extends the Job Demands-Resources (JD-R) model to include digital job demands (DJD) and digital job resources (DJR), testing their effects on strain and motivation among white-collar employees.

**Sources**: Scholze and Hecker - 2024.md

---

## Bibliographic Details

- **Author**: Alexander Scholze (UMIT TIROL) & Achim Hecker (DBU Digital Business University of Applied Sciences)
- **Year**: 2024
- **Journal**: Computers in Human Behavior, 155, 108177
- **Methodology**: Quantitative
- **Sample**: 898 white-collar employees from two financial industry companies
- **Analysis**: Partial Least Squares Structural Equation Modeling (PLS-SEM)
- **Data collection**: Online survey questionnaire (June-July 2022)

## Research Model

- **Independent variables**:
  - [[digital-job-demands]] (DJD): Availability (DICT), Work intensification (DICT), Technology dependence (DICT)
  - [[digital-job-resources]] (DJR): Collaboration (DICT), Efficiency (DICT), Autonomy (DICT)
  - [[job-demands]] (JD): Quantitative, Emotional, Work-privacy conflicts
  - [[job-resources]] (JR): Feedback, Job autonomy, Self-efficacy
- **Dependent variables**:
  - [[strain]]: Health, Burnout, Presenteeism
  - [[motivation]]: Engagement, Job satisfaction
  - Organizational implications (Turnover intentions, Attachment to workplace)
- **Moderator**: [[digital-job-demands]] (moderates the effect of DJR on motivation)

## Hypotheses and Results

*(Note: The paper does not explicitly state numbered hypotheses but investigates relationships based on the JD-R model).*

| Relationship | Result |
|---|---|
| DJD → Strain (+) | **Supported** (β = 0.233, p < .05) |
| DJR → Motivation (+) | **Supported** (β = 0.264, p < .05) |
| JD → Strain (+) | **Supported** (β = 0.466, p < .05) |
| JR → Motivation (+) | **Supported** (β = 0.509, p < .05) |
| Motivation → Organizational implications (+) | **Supported** (β = 0.797, p < .05) |
| Strain → Organizational implications (-) | **Not Supported** (β = -0.012, ns) |
| DJD * DJR → Motivation | **Supported** (Negative moderation, β = -0.063, p < .05) |

## Key Findings

### The "Dark Side" of Digitization
Digital job demands (DJD), operationalized as technology dependence, communication overload, and work intensification, act as stressors. The study demonstrates a significant positive association between DJD and psychological strain, highlighting that digitization has direct consequences for psychological well-being (source: Scholze and Hecker - 2024.md, p. 8).

### The "Bright Side" of Digitization
Digital job resources (DJR), including technology-enhanced collaboration, efficiency, and autonomy, act as motivators. The extended JD-R model confirms a strong positive correlation between digital job resources and motivation (source: Scholze and Hecker - 2024.md, p. 8). 

### Interaction Effects
The moderation analysis reveals that the effect of DJR on motivation is influenced by DJD. Specifically, the impact of digital job resources on motivation is affected by the level of digital job demands (source: Scholze and Hecker - 2024.md, p. 6). 

## Contradictions

The study noted contradictory results regarding DJD work intensification (DICT), suggesting it could act as both a hindrance and a challenge demand, pointing to the need for future research to classify DJD into hindrances and challenges (source: Scholze and Hecker - 2024.md, p. 8).

## Practical Implications

- **Digital technology optimization**: Organizations should prioritize user-friendliness and efficiency in digital tools, avoiding adding complexity. Workloads and technology-induced pressure should be monitored (source: Scholze and Hecker - 2024.md, p. 9).
- **Digital health protection**: Implement measures to enhance digital maturity, offer health programs, stress management, and consider "digital detox" initiatives (source: Scholze and Hecker - 2024.md, p. 9).
- **Digital communication**: Tools should facilitate collaboration efficiently. Policies limiting email and optimizing chat or virtual meetings can help reduce communication overload (source: Scholze and Hecker - 2024.md, p. 9).
- **Employee feedback**: Establish feedback sessions regarding digital working conditions to involve employees in the digital transformation process (source: Scholze and Hecker - 2024.md, p. 9).

## Limitation

- **Stated Limitations**: Cross-sectional design limits causal claims; possible self-selection bias; focuses solely on attitudes without considering personal characteristics that might influence strain or motivation; specific scales had to be shortened; limited generalizability as it focused on one industry (financial sector) (source: Scholze and Hecker - 2024.md, p. 9).
- **Unstated Limitations**: The study aggregates different types of digital demands and resources into higher-order constructs. This might obscure the specific, distinct mechanisms through which individual digital tools (e.g., Slack vs. specialized software) affect employees differently.

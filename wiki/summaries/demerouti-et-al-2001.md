# Demerouti et al. (2001) – The Job Demands-Resources Model of Burnout

**Summary**: This seminal paper introduces the Job Demands-Resources (JD-R) model, proposing that burnout develops through two processes: job demands leading to exhaustion, and a lack of job resources leading to disengagement.

**Sources**: Demerouti et al. - 2001.md

---

## Bibliographic Details

- **Author**: Evangelia Demerouti, Carl von Ossietzky University; Friedhelm Nachreiner, Carl von Ossietzky University; Arnold B. Bakker, Utrecht University; Wilmar B. Schaufeli, Utrecht University
- **Year**: 2001
- **Journal**: Journal of Applied Psychology, 86(3), 499-512
- **Methodology**: Quantitative
- **Sample**: 374 employees from three occupational fields in Germany (human services, industry, and transport)
- **Analysis**: Structural Equation Modeling (LISREL)
- **Data collection**: Self-report questionnaires and independent observer ratings of job conditions

## Research Model

- **Independent variable**: [[job-demands]] (physical workload, time pressure, recipient contact, physical environment, shift work)
- **Independent variable**: [[job-resources]] (feedback, rewards, job control, participation, job security, supervisor support)
- **Dependent variable**: [[burnout]] (Exhaustion, Disengagement) measured via the Oldenburg Burnout Inventory (OLBI).

## Hypotheses and Results

| Hypothesis | Description | Result |
|---|---|---|
| H1 | The two-factor structure of the OLBI is invariant across occupations within and outside the human services. | **Supported** (model fits well across all samples) |
| H2 | Job demands are primarily and positively related to exhaustion, whereas job resources are primarily and negatively related to disengagement from work. | **Supported** |
| H3 | Hypothesis 2 will hold for subjectively experienced job demands/resources as well as observer ratings. | **Supported** |
| H4 | The importance of specific job demands and resources will vary across occupational groups. | **Supported** |

## Key Findings

### Generalizability of Burnout
The study confirmed that burnout is not restricted to the human services sector. The core dimensions of exhaustion and disengagement were found across production, transport, and human services. The Oldenburg Burnout Inventory (OLBI) proved to be a valid instrument for assessing burnout regardless of the occupational context (source: Demerouti et al. - 2001.md, p. 508).

### The Two Processes of the JD-R Model
The Job Demands-Resources model proposes two parallel processes:
1. Demanding aspects of work lead to constant overtaxing and, ultimately, to **exhaustion** (source: Demerouti et al. - 2001.md, p. 502).
2. A lack of job resources complicates meeting job demands, leading to withdrawal behavior and, ultimately, **disengagement** from work (source: Demerouti et al. - 2001.md, p. 502).
The study found strong support for these two distinct pathways using both self-reported and observer-rated data.

### Occupational Differences
While the general framework of the JD-R model remained stable, the specific job demands and resources that contributed most heavily varied by occupation. For example, physical workload was the highest demand in human services and industry, while physical environment and shift work were most demanding in transport (source: Demerouti et al. - 2001.md, p. 507).

## Practical Implications

Workplace interventions aimed at preventing or reducing burnout should address specific job demands to avoid exhaustion, and increase job resources to enhance engagement. These interventions need to be tailored to the specific occupational group since the most important demands and resources vary by context (source: Demerouti et al. - 2001.md, p. 510).

## Limitation

The authors noted that the cross-sectional design of the study limits causal interpretations, though the correspondence between self-reports and observer ratings reduces common-method bias concerns. They also noted the sample was not randomly selected and instruments used to assess working conditions had a limited number of items (source: Demerouti et al. - 2001.md, p. 510).

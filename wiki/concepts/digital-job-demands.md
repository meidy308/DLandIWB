# Digital Job Demands

**Summary**: The physical, psychological, social, and organizational aspects of work that require sustained effort due to the utilization of Digital Information and Communication Technologies (DICT).

**Sources**: Scholze and Hecker - 2024.md

---

Digital Job Demands (DJD) extend the traditional concept of [[job-demands]] within the [[job-demands-resources-theory]] to encompass the unique stressors introduced by the "dark side" of digitization. These demands require physical and psychological exertion stemming from the continuous use of digital tools and connectivity (source: Scholze and Hecker - 2024.md, p. 3).

## Dimensions

Scholze and Hecker (2024) conceptualize DJD as a higher-order construct comprising the following lower-order dimensions:
- **Availability (DICT)**: Continuous accessibility and the expectation to be permanently responsive through digital channels (source: Scholze and Hecker - 2024.md, p. 4).
- **Work Intensification (DICT)**: The use of digital technologies leading to an increased volume or pace of work activities and heightened expectations for productivity (source: Scholze and Hecker - 2024.md, p. 4).
- **Technology Dependence (DICT)**: The reliance on digital tools to complete tasks, manage operations, communicate, and make decisions, creating stress when these tools fail or are unavailable (source: Scholze and Hecker - 2024.md, p. 4).

## Relationship with Other Variables

- **Strain (DV)**: DJD is positively associated with psychological strain, including burnout, presenteeism, and lower health. The continuous demands of digitization act as stressors that deplete employees' energy (source: Scholze and Hecker - 2024.md, p. 8).
- **Motivation (Moderation)**: DJD negatively moderates the positive relationship between [[digital-job-resources]] and motivation. The beneficial motivational effects of digital resources are dampened when digital demands are high (source: Scholze and Hecker - 2024.md, p. 6).
- **Technostress**: High levels of DJD contribute directly to [[technostress]], which encompasses the psychological strain induced by technology (source: Scholze and Hecker - 2024.md, p. 2).

## Measurement

Scholze and Hecker (2024) measured DJD using a 5-point Likert scale drawing from established measures:
- **Availability**: Adapted from Karr-Wisniewski and Lu (2010)'s Communication Overload scale.
- **Work Intensification**: Adapted from Ragu-Nathan et al. (2008)'s Techno-overload scale.
- **Technology Dependence**: Adapted from Karr-Wisniewski and Lu (2010)'s Technology dependence scale.

## Contradictions

There is a suggestion that elements of DJD, such as work intensification, may not be purely hindering. Some employees might evaluate these demands positively as [[challenge-demands]] rather than purely [[hindrance-demands]]. This indicates that the impact of DJD may vary based on individual appraisal and organizational context (source: Scholze and Hecker - 2024.md, p. 8).

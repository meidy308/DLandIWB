# Intrinsic Motivation

**Summary**: An internal drive to act out of interest, satisfaction, or a desire to achieve personal goals, serving as a critical engine for converting leadership support into innovative output.

**Sources**: Hastiti et al. - 2025.md

---

Intrinsic motivation originates from inside an individual and is critical for driving innovation. When employees feel intrinsically motivated, they become more committed, proactive, and resilient in the face of challenges (source: Hastiti et al. - 2025.md, p. 45). They explore new ideas without relying purely on external compensation, such as bonuses or promotions.

## Dimensions

Intrinsic motivation is characterized by several internal psychological states (source: Hastiti et al. - 2025.md, p. 45):
- **Autonomy** — The freedom to explore potential and make choices in the work environment.
- **Job Satisfaction** — Deriving pleasure from the work process itself rather than external rewards.
- **Passion to Excel** — An internal drive to continually improve and innovate despite facing obstacles.

## Relationship with Other Variables

### As a Mediator
Intrinsic motivation serves as a vital bridge between leadership style and innovative outcomes:
- **[[digital-leadership]] → Intrinsic Motivation → [[innovative-work-behavior]]** — In the higher education sector, digital leadership does not directly trigger innovation but works by strengthening the internal motivation of staff. Digital leaders foster this by offering autonomy and providing real-time feedback through technology (source: Hastiti et al. - 2025.md, p. 51-53).

### Moderation by Gender
Following [[social-role-theory]], research indicates that the impact of digital leadership on intrinsic motivation is **significantly stronger for women** (est = 0.902) than for men (est = 0.770) (source: Hastiti et al. - 2025.md, p. 52).

## Measurement

- **Hastiti et al. (2025)**: measured using 5 indicators focusing on job satisfaction without external incentives and the internal motivation to achieve. Measured on a 5-point Likert scale. Reliability: Cronbach's α = 0.838 (source: Hastiti et al. - 2025.md, p. 47-48).

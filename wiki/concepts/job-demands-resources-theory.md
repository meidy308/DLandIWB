# Job Demands-Resources Theory

**Summary**: A formal theoretical framework matured from the original JD-R model, explaining how job demands and job resources independently and interactively affect employee well-being, burnout, and performance through dual psychological processes and reciprocal spirals.

**Sources**: Demerouti et al. - 2001.md, Bakker et al. - 2003.md, Bakker and Demerouti - 2007.md, Bakker and Demerouti - 2014.md, Bakker and Demerouti - 2017.md, Crawford et al. - 2010.md, Afif and Fitrianingrum - 2025.md, Zia et al. - 2024.md, Demerouti - 2022.md

---

The Job Demands-Resources (JD-R) framework was foundationally introduced by Demerouti et al. (2001) and later expanded into a "state of the art" model by Bakker and Demerouti (2007). By 2014, the model matured into a formal theory, which was further consolidated in 2017 with the formalization of 8 key propositions (source: Bakker and Demerouti - 2017.md, p. 273).

## The 8 Propositions of JD-R Theory

The theory is defined by eight formal propositions that synthesize cumulative evidence (source: Bakker and Demerouti - 2017.md, p. 273-277):

1. **Classification (P1)**: All job characteristics can be classified into two broad categories: [[job-demands]] and [[job-resources]].
2. **Dual Processes (P2)**: Demands and resources instigate two different psychological processes: the [[health-impairment-process]] and the [[motivational-process]].
3. **Buffer Hypothesis (P3)**: [[job-resources]] can buffer the impact of job demands on strain and burnout.
4. **Saliency Hypothesis (P4)**: Job resources gain their motivating potential and become particularly useful when job demands are high.
5. **Personal Resources (P5)**: [[personal-resources]] (e.g., self-efficacy, optimism) play a similar role as job resources in direct and buffering effects.
6. **Performance Link (P6)**: Motivation has a positive impact on job performance, whereas job strain has a negative impact.
7. **Gain Spirals (P7)**: Engaged employees are likely to use [[job-crafting]] behaviors, creating higher levels of resources and even higher motivation.
8. **Loss Spirals (P8)**: Strained employees are likely to show [[self-undermining]] behaviors, leading to higher levels of demands and even higher strain.

## Theoretical Pillars

The maturation emphasized four key strengths of the theory:
1. **Flexibility**: Can be applied to all work environments by modeling characteristics as either demands or resources.
2. **Dual Processes**: Simultaneously considers stress and motivation.
3. **Dynamic Nature**: Acknowledges reversed causal effects and "spirals" through job crafting and self-undermining.
4. **Interventions**: Provides a taxonomy for individual and organizational-level changes, including the **JD-R Monitor** (source: Bakker and Demerouti - 2017.md, p. 281).

## Dual Processes

The theory proposes two distinct, parallel psychological processes:
1. **[[health-impairment-process]]**: Chronic job demands exhaust mental and physical resources, leading to [[exhaustion]] and burnout. This process is fueled by [[self-undermining]] behavior in later stages (Proposition 8).
2. **[[motivational-process]]**: Job resources fulfill basic psychological needs, leading to high [[work-engagement]] and excellent performance. This process is fueled by [[job-crafting]] behavior (Proposition 7).

## Interaction Effects

The theory highlights how demands and resources interact to predict occupational well-being:
- **[[buffer-hypothesis]]**: Resources mitigate the impact of demands on strain.
- **[[saliency-hypothesis]]**: Resources are most motivating when demands are high.
- **JD-JD Interactions**: Recent developments also suggest that job demands can interact with each other, accumulating stress (source: Bakker and Demerouti - 2017.md, p. 278).

## JD-R Interventions

Interventions are classified along two dimensions:

| Level | Environment-Target | Person-Target |
|---|---|---|
| **Organization** | Job Redesign / Organizational Assessment | Training (e.g., PsyCap) |
| **Individual** | [[job-crafting]] Interventions | Personal Resources / Strengths-based |
| **Technology** | User-Centric Redesign / Resource Maximization | [[meta-skills]] Training |

## JD-R in the Digital Age

The JD-R theory has been adapted to analyze the impact of [[digitalization-and-automation]] on the workforce. Demerouti (2022) proposes that technology's impact is determined by its design as either a demand or a resource.

### Turning Technology into a Resource
Digitalization contributes to healthy jobs when it is designed to support work rather than replace people, and when employees maintain control through [[job-crafting]]. A key risk identified is **[[digital-taylorism]]**, where technology leaves humans with only repetitive, high-vigilance "left-over" tasks (source: Demerouti - 2022.md, p. 1208).

### The Role of Design
- **Supportive Design**: Technology should facilitate decision-making and reduce costs of demands.
- **Shared Growth**: Impact is positive when economic gains from automation are shared with employees.
- **Protection**: Authorities must provide norms to prevent exploitation and overload (source: Demerouti - 2022.md, p. 1209).

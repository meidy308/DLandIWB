# Job Demands-Resources Theory

**Summary**: A formal theoretical framework matured from the original JD-R model, explaining how job demands and job resources independently and interactively affect employee well-being, burnout, and performance through dual psychological processes and reciprocal spirals.

**Sources**: Demerouti et al. - 2001.md, Bakker et al. - 2003.md, Bakker and Demerouti - 2007.md, Bakker and Demerouti - 2014.md, Crawford et al. - 2010.md, Afif and Fitrianingrum - 2025.md, Zia et al. - 2024.md

---

The Job Demands-Resources (JD-R) framework was foundationally introduced by Demerouti et al. (2001) and later expanded into a "state of the art" model by Bakker and Demerouti (2007). By 2014, the model matured into a formal theory characterized by its flexibility and focus on dynamic, emergent work roles (source: Bakker and Demerouti - 2014.md, p. 8).

## Theoretical Pillars

The 2014 maturation emphasized four key strengths of the theory over early models like DCM or ERI:
1. **Flexibility**: Can be applied to all work environments by modeling characteristics as either demands or resources (source: Bakker and Demerouti - 2014.md, p. 9).
2. **Dual Processes**: Simultaneously considers stress and motivation.
3. **Dynamic Nature**: Acknowledges reversed causal effects and "spirals" (loss and gain).
4. **Interventions**: Provides a taxonomy for individual and organizational-level changes.

## Dual Processes

The theory proposes two distinct, parallel psychological processes:
1. **[[health-impairment-process]]**: Chronic [[job-demands]] (e.g., work overload, emotional demands) exhaust mental and physical resources, leading to [[exhaustion]] and health problems (source: Bakker and Demerouti - 2014.md, p. 9).
2. **[[motivational-process]]**: [[job-resources]] fulfill basic psychological needs (autonomy, competence, relatedness), leading to high [[work-engagement]] and excellent performance (source: Bakker and Demerouti - 2014.md, p. 9).

## Interaction Effects

The theory highlights how demands and resources interact to predict occupational well-being:
- **[[buffer-hypothesis]]**: Job resources (e.g., social support, feedback) can mitigate the impact of job demands on strain and burnout (source: Bakker and Demerouti - 2014.md, p. 11).
- **[[saliency-hypothesis]]**: Job resources gain their motivational potential particularly when employees are confronted with high job demands (source: Bakker and Demerouti - 2014.md, p. 11).

## Reciprocal Relationships and Spirals

JD-R theory recognizes that the relationship between environment and well-being is not unidirectional:
- **Loss Spirals**: Exhausted employees may create or perceive more job demands over time, leading to a downward spiral of strain (source: Bakker and Demerouti - 2014.md, p. 14).
- **Gain Spirals**: Engaged employees mobilize their own job resources (e.g., asking for help, seeking feedback), leading to a "resource mobilization" effect that further boosts engagement (source: Bakker and Demerouti - 2014.md, p. 14-15).
- **[[job-crafting]]**: Serves as the primary behavioral mechanism through which employees proactively adjust their demands and resources (source: Bakker and Demerouti - 2014.md, p. 16-17).

## JD-R Interventions

Interventions are classified along two dimensions (source: Bakker and Demerouti - 2014.md, p. 18):

| Level | Environment-Target | Person-Target |
|---|---|---|
| **Organization** | Job Redesign | Training (e.g., PsyCap) |
| **Individual** | [[job-crafting]] | Strengths-based Interventions |

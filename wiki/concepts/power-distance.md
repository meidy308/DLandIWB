# Power Distance

**Summary**: A cultural value orientation representing the degree to which employees accept and expect unequal power distribution within an organization.

**Sources**: Li and Xiao - 2023.md

---

Power distance is an individual-level cultural value that determines how subordinates perceive and react to leadership behavior. It describes the acceptance of power imbalances between leaders and followers. In digital management contexts, it serves as a critical boundary condition that moderates how digital leadership cues are processed (source: Li and Xiao - 2023.md, p. 8).

## Dimensions and Characteristics

The study distinguishes between employees based on their power distance orientation (source: Li and Xiao - 2023.md, p. 8, 16):

- **High Power Distance**:
    - Psychologically far from leaders.
    - Believes in formal authority and obedience.
    - Insensitive to leaders' digital communication and "caring" signals.
    - Less likely to challenge the status quo or provide innovative feedback.
- **Low Power Distance**:
    - Psychologically close to leaders.
    - Values equality and open communication.
    - Highly sensitive to digital communication and cares about leaders' feedback.
    - More responsive to supportive leadership and likely to engage in collaborative innovation.

## Relationship with Other Variables

### As Moderator
- **[[digital-leadership]] → [[psychological-capital]]**: Power distance significantly and negatively moderates this relationship ($B = -0.116, p < .05$). In high power distance settings, digital leadership has a lower impact on building employees' stores of psychological resources (source: Li and Xiao - 2023.md, p. 11, 13).
- **[[digital-leadership]] → [[affective-commitment-to-leadership]]**: Power distance also negatively moderates the link between digital leadership and emotional commitment ($B = -0.154, p < .01$). High power distance orientation dampens the formation of emotional bonds triggered by digital leadership (source: Li and Xiao - 2023.md, p. 11, 13).

## Measurement

Li and Xiao (2023) utilized the individual power distance unidimensional scale developed by Earley and Erez (1987), consisting of 8 items on a 5-point Likert scale (source: Li and Xiao - 2023.md, p. 11). Example items:
- "In most circumstances, leaders can make decisions alone without consulting subordinates."
- "Leaders have the power to urge subordinates to obey in work issues."

# Digital Taylorism

**Summary**: A work design risk where digitalization and automation take over complex and stimulating tasks, leaving human employees with repetitive, vigilant, or fragmented roles.

**Sources**: Demerouti - 2022.md

---

Digital Taylorism refers to the application of Taylorist principles (fragmentation of tasks, strict control, and focus on efficiency) in the digital age through the use of algorithms and automation. It often results from a "left-over" design principle where technology handles the treatment, manipulation, or assembly of information, leaving the human operator with vigilant roles (sustained attention to visual stimuli) or maintenance tasks (source: Demerouti - 2022.md, p. 1206, 1208).

## Dimensions

- **Task Fragmentation**: Breaking down complex digital work into small, repetitive sub-tasks.
- **Monitoring and Control**: Using digital tools to track employee performance with high granularity.
- **Reduced Autonomy**: Decision-making is shifted from the employee to the algorithm or system.

## Relationship with Other Variables

- **[[job-demands]]**: Digital Taylorism increases cognitive demands like vigilance while reducing stimulating demands (source: Demerouti - 2022.md, p. 1206).
- **[[job-resources]]**: It typically leads to a decrease in autonomy and task significance, which are core job resources.
- **[[burnout]]**: The lack of resources and increased vigilance demands can lead to exhaustion and health impairment.
- **[[work-engagement]]**: By reducing the meaningfulness of work, it negatively impacts employee engagement (source: Demerouti - 2022.md, p. 1208).

## Measurement

While not explicitly measured in Demerouti (2022), it is often assessed through scales measuring task variety, autonomy, and digital monitoring intensity.

## Contradictions

Some argue that technology enriches jobs by removing "dirty, dull, or dangerous" tasks. However, Demerouti (2022) notes that without proactive design, the "left-over" tasks for humans may still be dull and highly demanding in terms of mental vigilance.

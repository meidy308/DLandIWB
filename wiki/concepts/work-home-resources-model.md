# Work-Home Resources Model

**Summary**: An integrative framework that explains how demands and resources in one domain (work or home) influence outcomes in the other through volatile personal resources.

**Sources**: Bakker et al. - 2023.md

---

The Work-Home Resources (WH-R) model (Ten Brummelhuis & Bakker, 2012) integrates spillover and crossover theories with the [[job-demands-resources-theory]]. It proposes that job characteristics and home characteristics affect each other through changes in personal resources such as time, mood, and energy (source: Bakker et al. - 2023.md, p. 41).

## Dimensions

The model operates through several key mechanisms:
- **Spillover**: Demands/resources in one domain affect outcomes in the same person in another domain (e.g., job stress leading to poor family performance).
- **Crossover**: Feelings or states in one person affect a significant other in another domain (e.g., an engaged employee making their partner happier at home) (source: Bakker et al. - 2023.md, p. 41).
- **Volatile Resources**: Short-term personal resources like mood, energy, and physical stamina that fluctuate daily.
- **Macro/Key Resources**: Stable factors like culture, personality, and skills that moderate the spillover process (source: Bakker et al. - 2023.md, p. 41).

## Relationship with Other Variables

- **Work to Home**: Job demands can lead to exhaustion, which increases withdrawal behaviors at home. Conversely, personal accomplishment at work can improve family performance (source: Bakker et al. - 2023.md, p. 41).
- **Home to Work**: Positive family events can moderate the relationship between daily job demands and [[job-performance]] by providing a resourceful home life that facilitates coping (source: Bakker et al. - 2023.md, p. 41).
- **Moderation**: High-resource home environments (e.g., positive child-related events) help employees better handle high job demands the next day (source: Bakker et al. - 2023.md, p. 41).

## Measurement

Typically measured using daily diary or experience sampling methods focusing on cross-domain effects of demands and resources (source: Bakker et al. - 2023.md, p. 41).

# Multilevel JD-R Theory

**Summary**: An extension of JD-R theory that acknowledges individuals are nested in teams, which are nested in organizations, and explains how characteristics at higher levels trickle down to influence individual well-being and performance.

**Sources**: Bakker et al. - 2023.md

---

Multilevel JD-R Theory (Bakker, 2015) provides a structural framework for understanding how organizational-level factors (like HR practices and climate) and team-level factors (like shared leadership and team resources) impact individual-level demands, resources, and well-being (source: Bakker et al. - 2023.md, p. 38).

## Dimensions

The theory considers three primary levels (source: Bakker et al. - 2023.md, p. 38, Figure 3):
- **Organizational Level**: Strategic HRM, organizational climate, and job design policies.
- **Leader/Team Level**: Leadership behaviors (e.g., transformational leadership, empowering leadership), team-level job demands/resources, and team well-being.
- **Individual Level**: Personal perceptions of job demands/resources, individual well-being ([[work-engagement]], [[burnout]]), and behaviors ([[job-crafting]], [[self-undermining]]).

## Relationship with Other Variables

- **Trickle-down Effects**: Organizational climate signals management's priorities. A high **psychosocial safety climate** leads to better job design, higher engagement, and lower strain (source: Bakker et al. - 2023.md, p. 39).
- **Leadership as a Resource**: Leaders can directly provide resources (feedback, support) or indirectly influence demands (taking away hindrance demands) (source: Bakker et al. - 2023.md, p. 39).
- **Team-level Dynamics**: Team members share perceptions of demands and resources. **Team job crafting** can instigate individual job crafting and increase individual performance (source: Bakker et al. - 2023.md, p. 39).
- **Emotional Contagion**: Interdependent team members may influence each other's affect and behavior through emotional contagion (source: Bakker et al. - 2023.md, p. 39).

## Measurement

Requires multilevel modeling (e.g., HLM or MSEM) to account for the nested structure of data. Levels can be measured through objective raters (at the occupation/team level) or aggregated individual perceptions (source: Bakker et al. - 2023.md, p. 39).

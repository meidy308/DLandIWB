# Cognitive-Affective Processing System (CAPS)

**Summary**: A psychological framework explaining that individual behavior originates from the interaction between external situational factors and internal cognitive-emotional reactions.

**Sources**: Gao and Gao - 2024.md

---

The Cognitive-Affective Processing System (CAPS) framework, originally proposed by Mischel and Shoda (1995), posits that individuals do not just respond to situations directly, but rather process them through a complex network of cognitive-affective units. In the context of organizational behavior, this framework helps researchers explain the "black box" between leadership styles and employee outcomes (source: Gao and Gao - 2024.md, p. 2).

## Core Principles

The CAPS framework is built on several key propositions (source: Gao and Gao - 2024.md, p. 2, 21):
- **Situational Stimuli**: External factors (e.g., [[digital-leadership]]) serve as inputs to the system.
- **Dual Processing Paths**: Individuals evaluate these stimuli through both a **Cognitive Path** (mental evaluations, meanings, and beliefs) and an **Affective Path** (emotional reactions and feelings).
- **Behavioral Manifestation**: The resulting behavior (e.g., [[innovative-work-behavior]]) is a product of how these internal units interact.
- **Trait Moderation**: Individual differences (e.g., [[proactive-personality]]) can influence how situational cues are processed, though findings vary across contexts.

## Relationship with Other Variables

### As a Research Framework
In the study by Gao & Gao (2024), CAPS was used to model the dual mediating role of empowerment and commitment:

- **Cognitive Mediator**: [[psychological-empowerment]] was identified as the cognitive unit activated by digital leadership, leading to innovation (source: Gao and Gao - 2024.md, p. 16).
- **Affective Mediator**: [[affective-commitment]] was identified as the affective unit, though its path to innovation was found to be weaker in a digital transformation context (source: Gao and Gao - 2024.md, p. 20).
- **Interaction**: The framework illustrates that focusing exclusively on either cognition or affect provides an incomplete picture of how leadership drives behavior.

# Transactional Theory of Stress

**Summary**: A psychological framework (Lazarus & Folkman, 1984) positing that the impact of a stressor is determined by an individual's appraisal of its significance for their well-being.

**Sources**: Crawford et al. - 2010.md

---

The Transactional Theory of Stress suggests that stress is not an inherent property of a situation, but a "transaction" between the person and the environment. The core mechanism is the **appraisal process**, where individuals evaluate whether a demand is a threat, a challenge, or a hindrance (source: Crawford et al. - 2010.md, p. 836).

## Dimensions

### Primary Appraisal
The initial evaluation of whether a stressor is relevant and what its potential impact is:
- **Challenge Appraisal**: Perceiving a demand as having potential for gain, mastery, or growth.
- **Hindrance/Threat Appraisal**: Perceiving a demand as having potential for harm, loss, or blockage of goals.

### Secondary Appraisal
Evaluating what can be done to cope with the stressor (coping resources and options).

(source: Crawford et al. - 2010.md, p. 836)

## Relationship with Other Variables

- **Link to [[job-demands-resources-theory]]**: Crawford et al. (2010) integrated this theory into the JD-R model to differentiate [[challenge-demands]] from [[hindrance-demands]]. This integration explains why different demands lead to different levels of [[work-engagement]] (source: Crawford et al. - 2010.md, p. 837).
- **Emotions and Coping**:
    - **Challenge appraisals** trigger positive emotions (excitement) and active, problem-focused coping (source: Crawford et al. - 2010.md, p. 837).
    - **Hindrance appraisals** trigger negative emotions (anxiety, anger) and passive, emotion-focused coping or withdrawal (source: Crawford et al. - 2010.md, p. 837).

## Measurement

In the context of JD-R research, this theory is often tested using self-report measures where participants rate their perceptions of specific job demands as either challenging or hindering (source: Crawford et al. - 2010.md, p. 839).

## Contradictions

The theory acknowledges that individual differences affect appraisal; however, work contexts often evoke fairly consistent appraisals across individuals for certain types of stressors (source: Crawford et al. - 2010.md, p. 836).

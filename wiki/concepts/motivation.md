# Motivation

**Summary**: A positive psychological state and internal drive resulting from the availability of job resources, encompassing work engagement and job satisfaction.

**Sources**: Scholze and Hecker - 2024.md

---

Within the [[job-demands-resources-theory]], motivation is the primary outcome of the [[motivational-process]]. The availability of [[job-resources]] fulfills basic psychological needs (such as autonomy, competence, and relatedness), leading to higher levels of motivation, engagement, and commitment to the organization.

## Dimensions

Motivation is often conceptualized as a broader category containing specific positive organizational behaviors and attitudes, including:
- **[[work-engagement]]** (vigor, dedication, and absorption)
- **Job Satisfaction** (contentment with various aspects of the job, such as career prospects, colleagues, and management)
- **[[intrinsic-motivation]]** (internal drive to perform tasks)

## Relationship with Other Variables

- **[[job-resources]] (Antecedent)**: The availability of resources (e.g., feedback, autonomy) strongly predicts higher motivation.
- **[[digital-job-resources]] (Antecedent)**: In the context of digitization, digital resources (like enhanced collaboration and digital autonomy) are strongly and positively correlated with employee motivation, representing the "bright side" of digitization (source: Scholze and Hecker - 2024.md, p. 8).
- **[[digital-job-demands]] (Moderator)**: The positive effect of digital job resources on motivation is moderated by digital job demands. When digital demands are high, the motivational impact of digital resources can become even more significant, though overwhelming demands may also interfere (source: Scholze and Hecker - 2024.md, p. 6).
- **Organizational Implications (Consequence)**: High motivation leads to positive organizational outcomes, such as stronger attachment to the workplace and reduced turnover intentions (source: Scholze and Hecker - 2024.md, p. 6).

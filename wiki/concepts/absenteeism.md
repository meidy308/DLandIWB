# Absenteeism

**Summary**: The failure of an employee to report for scheduled work, often operationalized through frequency or duration of sick leave.

**Sources**: Bakker et al. - 2003.md

---

Absenteeism represents a significant organizational challenge, causing operational disruptions and financial losses. While absenteeism can result from numerous causes, it is frequently studied as an outcome of health impairment and psychological strain in the workplace (source: Bakker et al. - 2003.md, p. 396-397).

## Relationship with Other Variables

In the context of the [[job-demands-resources-theory]]:
- **[[exhaustion]] and [[repetitive-strain-injury]]**: According to the health impairment process, high [[job-demands]] lead to physical and psychological health problems (like exhaustion and RSI). These health issues, in turn, positively predict absenteeism. In Bakker et al. (2003), health problems were a significant predictor of self-reported absence duration and long-term absence (β = .25, p < .01) (source: Bakker et al. - 2003.md, p. 405).
- Job demands do not typically have a direct effect on absenteeism; rather, health problems act as a conditional variable bridging job demands and absence behavior (source: Bakker et al. - 2003.md, p. 409).

## Measurement

Absenteeism is typically measured either via organizational personnel records or self-reports. 
In Bakker et al. (2003), it was measured through self-report with two items:
- **Absence Duration**: "During the past 12 months, how many working days did you not work because of ill health?"
- **Long-term Absence**: "During the past 12 months, have you been sick longer than 2 weeks in a row one or more times?" (source: Bakker et al. - 2003.md, p. 401).

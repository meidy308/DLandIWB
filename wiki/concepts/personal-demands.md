# Personal Demands

**Summary**: Internal requirements individuals set for their own performance and behavior that require sustained effort and are associated with psychological costs.

**Sources**: Bakker and Demerouti - 2017.md

---

Personal demands are "the requirements that individuals set for their own performance and behavior that force them to invest effort in their work and are therefore associated with physical and psychological costs" (source: Bakker and Demerouti - 2017.md, p. 279). While [[job-demands]] originate from the environment, personal demands originate from within the individual.

## Dimensions

Examples of personal demands include:
- **Workaholism**: An internal drive to work excessively hard (source: Bakker and Demerouti - 2017.md, p. 279).
- **Perfectionism**: Setting extremely high standards for oneself (source: Bakker and Demerouti - 2017.md, p. 279).
- **High Performance Expectations**: Internalized goals regarding one's own productivity (source: Bakker and Demerouti - 2017.md, p. 279).

## Relationship with Other Variables

### Health-Impairment Process
Like job demands, certain personal demands (e.g., workaholism) act as risk factors that contribute to [[burnout]] and ill-health independently of the job context (source: Bakker and Demerouti - 2017.md, p. 279).

### Motivational Process
Some personal demands may act as "internal challenge demands." For example, high performance expectations can predict higher levels of future [[work-engagement]] as they trigger employees to increase effort to meet those expectations (source: Bakker and Demerouti - 2017.md, p. 279).

## Measurement

Personal demands are measured through personality traits or behavioral tendency scales, such as:
- Dutch Work Addiction Scale (DUWAS) for workaholism.
- Perfectionism scales focusing on self-oriented standards.
- Performance expectation items (e.g., Barbier et al., 2013).

## Contradictions

There is ongoing discussion regarding whether a specific characteristic is a demand or a resource. For example, high expectations can be a "demand" (costing energy) or a "motivational force" depending on the individual's resilience and the presence of supporting [[job-resources]] (source: Bakker and Demerouti - 2017.md, p. 279).

# Social Identity Theory

**Summary**: A theoretical framework suggesting that individuals define themselves based on the social groups they belong to and that this sense of belonging influences their behavior to align with group values.

**Sources**: Khan et al. - 2025.md

---

Social identity theory (SIT), building on the work of Turner et al. (1979) and Ashforth and Mael (1989), suggests that people’s behaviors and sense of identity are shaped by the social groups they belong to, including organizations (source: Khan et al. - 2025.md, p. 5). In the context of the workplace, as employees identify more strongly with their organization, they tend to adopt behaviors that reflect the organization's core values.

## Dimensions

- **Categorization**: Mentally placing people (including oneself) into groups.
- **Identification**: Associating oneself with certain groups (the "in-group").
- **Comparison**: Contrasting the "in-group" with "out-groups" to maintain self-esteem.

## Relationship with Other Variables

In the research by [[khan-et-al-2025]], SIT provides the underlying logic for:
- **Green Organizational Climate**: Explains why a supportive climate for sustainability pushes employees toward eco-friendly behaviors. Employees internalize environmental missions as part of their own identity (source: Khan et al. - 2025.md, p. 5).
- **Green Innovative Behavior**: When organizations create strong green climates, employees show higher commitment to sustainable initiatives, driven by a shared sense of purpose and organizational belonging (source: Khan et al. - 2025.md, p. 5).
- **Digital Leadership**: Leaders modeling sustainable values help shape an environment where [[green-innovative-behavior]] feels expected as part of the organizational identity (source: Khan et al. - 2025.md, p. 5).

# Strain

**Summary**: The negative psychological and physiological outcomes resulting from prolonged exposure to job demands, often encompassing burnout, health issues, and presenteeism.

**Sources**: Scholze and Hecker - 2024.md

---

Within the [[job-demands-resources-theory]], strain represents the outcome of the [[health-impairment-process]]. When employees are exposed to sustained [[job-demands]] without adequate [[job-resources]] to buffer their effects, they experience depletion of energy and subsequent psychological strain.

## Dimensions

Strain is often measured as a composite or higher-order construct encompassing:
- **[[burnout]]** (including exhaustion)
- **Health** (general physical and mental well-being)
- **Presenteeism** (attending work while sick, leading to reduced productivity and worsening health)

## Relationship with Other Variables

- **[[job-demands]] (Antecedent)**: High job demands are the primary predictor of strain.
- **[[digital-job-demands]] (Antecedent)**: The "dark side" of digitization introduces new digital demands (e.g., technology dependence, communication overload) that are significantly and positively associated with strain, such as [[technostress]] (source: Scholze and Hecker - 2024.md, p. 8).
- **Organizational Implications**: High strain can lead to negative organizational outcomes, though some studies show complex or non-significant direct effects on turnover intentions depending on the context (source: Scholze and Hecker - 2024.md, p. 6).
- **[[buffer-hypothesis]]**: [[job-resources]] and [[digital-job-resources]] can mitigate the impact of demands on strain.

# Self-Determination Theory

**Summary**: A theory of motivation that posits humans have three innate psychological needs—autonomy, competence, and relatedness—the satisfaction of which drives intrinsic motivation and well-being.

**Sources**: Huang et al. - 2026.md

---

Self-Determination Theory (SDT), developed by Ryan and Deci (2000), suggests that intrinsic motivation is a primary driver of innovative behavior. In the digital context, leaders play a critical role in fostering this motivation by satisfying core psychological needs (source: Huang et al. - 2026.md, p. 2).

## Core Needs

- **Autonomy**: The need to feel in control of one's own behaviors and goals. Digital leadership provides this through flexible work arrangements and self-management opportunities (source: Huang et al. - 2026.md, p. 3).
- **Competence**: The need to gain mastery and learn different skills. Digital leadership fosters this by providing cutting-edge digital tools and resources (source: Huang et al. - 2026.md, p. 3).
- **Relatedness**: The need to experience a sense of belonging and attachment to other people. Digital communication tools build social networks and team cohesion (source: Huang et al. - 2026.md, p. 3).

## Application in Innovation Research

SDT explains why certain leadership styles, like [[digital-leadership]], are effective at promoting [[innovative-work-behavior]]. By fulfilling these innate needs, leaders empower employees to engage in [[job-crafting]] and build [[innovation-efficacy]], which are precursors to creative output (source: Huang et al. - 2026.md, p. 2-3).

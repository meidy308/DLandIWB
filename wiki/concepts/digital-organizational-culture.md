# Digital Organizational Culture

**Summary**: The shared values, beliefs, and practices that shape how digital technologies are integrated into the workplace.

**Sources**: Çetinkaya and Sürücü - 2025.md, Deviren - 2026.md

---

Digital organizational culture reflects the extent to which a company embraces digital technology, agility, and innovation. It encompasses the common ideas, values, conventions, behaviors, and practices that influence how people engage with digital platforms and technology (source: Çetinkaya and Sürücü - 2025.md, p. 236).

## Dimensions

### Çetinkaya and Sürücü (2025) framework
Key characteristics of a strong digital organizational culture (source: Çetinkaya and Sürücü - 2025.md, p. 238):

1. **Openness to change** — Encouraging flexibility and the adoption of new technologies.
2. **Collaboration** — Emphasis on teamwork and communication across digital platforms.
3. **Continuous learning** — Promotion of ongoing education and skill development.
4. **Employee empowerment** — Granting employees autonomy to explore and implement innovative solutions.

### Deviren (2026) framework
Digital culture measured across five subdimensions (source: Deviren - 2026.md, p. 36):

1. **Digital intelligence/leadership** — Understanding and steering digital transformation.
2. **Adaptation** — Organizational agility in responding to digital change.
3. **Human capital** — Investing in people's digital competencies.
4. **Technology** — Effective integration of digital technologies.
5. **Collaboration** — Cross-functional teamwork in digital initiatives.

## Relationship with Other Variables

### As mediator (Çetinkaya & Sürücü, 2025)
Digital organizational culture serves as a **significant positive mediator** in the DL → IWB relationship:

- [[digital-leadership]] significantly predicts digital organizational culture (β = 0.452, t = 7.851, p < 0.05) (source: Çetinkaya and Sürücü - 2025.md, p. 242).
- Digital organizational culture significantly predicts [[innovative-work-behavior]] (β = 0.213, t = 5.641, p < 0.05) (source: Çetinkaya and Sürücü - 2025.md, p. 242).
- The indirect effect through digital culture is significant (β = 0.144, CI [0.088, 0.216]), meaning digital leaders shape a supportive, technologically agile culture that catalyzes employees to translate ideas into tangible innovations (source: Çetinkaya and Sürücü - 2025.md, p. 242–243).

### As moderator (Deviren, 2026)
In the automotive sector, digital culture plays a **negative moderating role**:

- Digital culture has strong positive direct effects on both IWB (B = 1.14) and [[adaptive-performance]] (B = 0.79) (source: Deviren - 2026.md, p. 39–40).
- However, as digital culture increases, the effect of [[digital-leadership]] on IWB and adaptive performance significantly decreases (B = −0.15 and −0.10, respectively) (source: Deviren - 2026.md, p. 39–40).
- This is interpreted as a **substitution effect** — when digital culture is already strong, it absorbs or replaces the contribution that digital leadership would otherwise make. Conversely, when digital culture is weak, the influence of digital leadership is substantially stronger (source: Deviren - 2026.md, p. 42–43).

## Measurement

Digital organizational culture is measured with different scales across the studies:

- **Çetinkaya and Sürücü (2025)**: 4 items, 5-point Likert (Zhen et al., 2021). Reliability: Cronbach's α = 0.879, CR = 0.891, McDonald's ω = 0.881, AVE = 0.673 (source: Çetinkaya and Sürücü - 2025.md, p. 240).
- **Deviren (2026)**: 23 items across 5 subdimensions, 5-point Likert (Digital Transformation Culture Assessment Scale) (source: Deviren - 2026.md, p. 36).

## Contradictions

- In **Çetinkaya and Sürücü (2025)**, digital organizational culture acts as a **positive mediator** between digital leadership and IWB — it bridges and amplifies DL's effect.
- In **Deviren (2026)**, digital culture **negatively moderates** the DL → IWB relationship — when already strong, it diminishes DL's marginal contribution.
- These are different statistical roles (mediator vs. moderator) and the findings are not directly contradictory in a statistical sense, but they present different narratives: one positions digital culture as a bridge amplifying DL's effect, while the other suggests it can substitute for DL when already established. Context differences (IT sector vs. automotive sector) and measurement differences (4-item vs. 23-item scale) may account for this divergence.

# Digital Organizational Culture

**Summary**: The shared values, beliefs, and practices that shape how digital technologies are integrated into the workplace.

**Sources**: Çetinkaya and Sürücü - 2025.md, Deviren - 2026.md

---

Digital organizational culture reflects the extent to which a company embraces digital technology, agility, and innovation. Key characteristics include an openness to change, strong collaboration via digital platforms, an emphasis on continuous learning, and employee empowerment (source: Çetinkaya and Sürücü - 2025.md, p. 238).

It serves as a critical mediating catalyst. Initiated by strong [[digital-leadership]], a positive digital culture helps translate new ideas into tangible outcomes. By valuing experimentation and viewing failure as a learning opportunity, the culture unleashes the creative potential of the workforce, resulting in increased [[innovative-work-behavior]] (source: Çetinkaya and Sürücü - 2025.md, p. 242-243).

In the automotive sector, digital culture has strong positive direct effects on both IWB (B = 1.14) and [[adaptive-performance]] (B = 0.79). However, it also plays a **negative moderating role**: as digital culture increases, the effect of [[digital-leadership]] on IWB and adaptive performance significantly decreases (B = −0.15 and −0.10, respectively). This is interpreted as a **substitution effect** — when digital culture is already strong, it absorbs or replaces the contribution that digital leadership would otherwise make. Conversely, when digital culture is weak, the influence of digital leadership is substantially stronger (source: Deviren - 2026.md, p. 39–40, 42–43).

The Deviren (2026) study measured digital culture using a Digital Transformation Culture Assessment Scale with five subdimensions: digital intelligence/leadership, adaptation, human capital, technology, and collaboration (23 items) (source: Deviren - 2026.md, p. 36).

## Contradictions

- In Çetinkaya and Sürücü (2025), digital organizational culture acts as a **positive mediator** between digital leadership and IWB. In contrast, Deviren (2026) finds that digital culture **negatively moderates** the DL → IWB relationship. These are different statistical roles (mediator vs. moderator) and the findings are not directly contradictory in a statistical sense, but they present different narratives: one positions digital culture as a bridge amplifying DL's effect, while the other suggests it can diminish DL's marginal contribution when already established.

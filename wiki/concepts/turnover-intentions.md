# Turnover Intentions

**Summary**: An employee's conscious and deliberate cognitive formulation to leave the organization in the near future.

**Sources**: Bakker et al. - 2003.md

---

Turnover intentions are the most salient and immediate precursors to actual withdrawal behavior from an organization. Understanding and reducing turnover intentions is crucial for organizations aiming to retain their workforce and lower recruitment and training costs.

## Relationship with Other Variables

In the context of the [[job-demands-resources-theory]]:
- **Involvement ([[affective-commitment]] & [[dedication]])**: In the motivational process of the JD-R model, adequate [[job-resources]] foster high involvement (commitment and dedication). High involvement is a strong negative predictor of turnover intentions (β = -.77, p < .01). Involvement acts as a full mediator between job resources and the intention to quit (source: Bakker et al. - 2003.md, p. 405, 409).
- **Health Problems ([[exhaustion]] & [[repetitive-strain-injury]])**: While turnover intentions are primarily driven by a lack of motivational resources, severe health problems or job strain may directly result in psychological withdrawal over and above the impact of low involvement. Bakker et al. (2003) found a small but significant positive relationship between health problems and turnover intentions (β = .18, p < .01) (source: Bakker et al. - 2003.md, p. 406, 410).

## Measurement

Turnover intentions are often assessed using self-report scales. In Bakker et al. (2003), it was assessed with a three-item scale by Schaufeli and Bakker, including items such as "I intend to change jobs during the next year" (measured on a 5-point Likert scale) (source: Bakker et al. - 2003.md, p. 401).

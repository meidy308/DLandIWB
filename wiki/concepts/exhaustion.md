# Exhaustion

**Summary**: The depletion of mental resources and the experience of severe fatigue, often resulting from prolonged exposure to high job demands.

**Sources**: Bakker et al. - 2003.md

---

Exhaustion is the core symptom of the [[burnout]] syndrome, referring specifically to the depletion of an individual's mental and physical resources. It develops when employees are repeatedly exposed to high work pressure and demanding environments without adequate time to recover (source: Bakker et al. - 2003.md, p. 396).

## Relationship with Other Variables

In the context of the [[job-demands-resources-theory]]:
- **[[job-demands]]**: The health impairment process of the JD-R model establishes that job demands (such as work pressure, emotional demands, task changes, and computer problems) are the primary predictors of exhaustion. These demands evoke a physiological and psychological energy depletion process (source: Bakker et al. - 2003.md, p. 396, 409).
- **[[absenteeism]]**: Exhaustion acts as a critical link (or conditional variable) between job demands and sickness absenteeism. Severe fatigue caused by overexertion directly leads to higher frequencies and durations of absence behavior (source: Bakker et al. - 2003.md, p. 397, 409).
- **[[turnover-intentions]]**: While primarily predicting absenteeism, exhaustion and overall health problems can also have a direct, albeit smaller, positive impact on an employee's intention to leave the organization, indicating a form of psychological withdrawal alongside physical withdrawal (source: Bakker et al. - 2003.md, p. 410).

## Measurement

Exhaustion is commonly measured using the Maslach Burnout Inventory—General Survey (MBI-GS). The exhaustion subscale includes items such as "I feel emotionally drained from my work," measured on a frequency scale (e.g., 0 = never, 6 = every day) (source: Bakker et al. - 2003.md, p. 400).

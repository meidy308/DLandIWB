# Dynamic Capabilities Theory

**Summary**: A strategic framework that explains how firms sustain competitive advantage by continuously developing, renewing, and reconfiguring internal and external competencies to respond to rapid environmental changes.

**Sources**: Umayna et al. - 2025.md

---

Dynamic Capabilities Theory (DCT) emerged as an advancement of the [[resource-based-theory]] (RBV/RBT). While RBV emphasizes owning valuable and rare resources, DCT addresses how firms *adapt* those resources in volatile markets (source: Umayna et al. - 2025.md, p. 135).

## Dimensions

DCT identifies three core capabilities:
1.  **Sensing**: The ability to detect changes, identify opportunities, and anticipate threats in the external environment.
2.  **Seizing**: Responding to insights through resource mobilization and strategic investment.
3.  **Reconfiguring**: The internal capacity to restructure operations, processes, or organizational design to maintain alignment with new directions (source: Umayna et al. - 2025.md, p. 135-136).

## Relationship with Other Variables

In the context of digital transformation and innovation:
- **[[innovative-work-behavior]]** and **[[problem-solving-creativity]]** are viewed as micro-foundations of **sensing** and **seizing** (source: Umayna et al. - 2025.md, p. 136).
- **[[digital-leadership]]** plays a pivotal role in enabling **reconfiguring**, allowing organizations to orchestrate resources dynamically into innovative outputs (source: Umayna et al. - 2025.md, p. 136, 145).
- **[[psychological-empowerment]]** acts as an enabling mechanism that enhances the internal capabilities needed for sensing and seizing (source: Umayna et al. - 2025.md, p. 141).

## Measurement

Measurement usually involves scales assessing a firm's agility, reconfiguration capability, and market responsiveness.

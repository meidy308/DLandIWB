# Social Role Theory

**Summary**: A grand theory positing that behavioral and psychological differences between men and women are largely influenced by socially constructed gender roles and societal expectations.

**Sources**: Hastiti et al. - 2025.md

---

Social role theory helps explain how societal expectations surrounding gender shape an individual's reaction to different work environments and leadership styles (source: Hastiti et al. - 2025.md, p. 44). Within the workplace, expectations tied to gender roles can alter the dynamics of creativity, motivation, and leadership reception.

## Relationship with Other Variables

### As a Moderator (Gender Role Congruity)
Social role theory is used to explain why digital leadership impacts genders differently (source: Hastiti et al. - 2025.md, p. 52):
- **Digital Leadership & [[intrinsic-motivation]]** — Expectations tied to social roles can make women more responsive to the motivational support provided by digital leaders, resulting in a stronger positive impact (est = 0.902) compared to men (est = 0.770).
- **Digital Leadership & [[problem-solving-creativity]]** — Conversely, male social roles may focus more on logical problem-solving in technological contexts, leading to a stronger path from digital leadership to creative problem-solving (est = 0.890) compared to women (est = 0.761).

## Dimensions

In the research by Hastiti et al. (2025), social role theory is applied across:
- **Communal Roles** (often associated with women) — Focus on interpersonal sensitivity and psychological support.
- **Agentic Roles** (often associated with men) — Focus on independence, logic, and problem-solving.

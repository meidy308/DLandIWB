# Repetitive Strain Injury

**Summary**: A form of Work Related Upper Limb Disorder (WRULD) characterized by damage and pain in the hands, arms, shoulders, or neck caused or exacerbated by aspects of the physical working situation.

**Sources**: Bakker et al. - 2003.md

---

Repetitive Strain Injury (RSI) is an umbrella term associated with pain, stiffness, or loss of power in the wrist and forearm/elbow area. It frequently occurs among employees who carry out repetitive tasks with their hands or arms without adequate rest breaks, such as computer and telephone workers in call centres (source: Bakker et al. - 2003.md, p. 396).

## Relationship with Other Variables

In the context of the [[job-demands-resources-theory]] and call centre environments:
- **[[job-demands]]**: The incidence of RSI is increased by a poor working environment and high levels of quantitative and technical demands (e.g., high workload, computer problems). These demands force the employee to work nonstop, leading to overexertion and pain. Thus, RSI acts as a physical indicator of health impairment driven by high job demands (source: Bakker et al. - 2003.md, p. 396-397).
- **[[absenteeism]]**: Alongside [[exhaustion]], RSI serves as a conditional variable linking job demands to absenteeism. Employees suffering from significant physical pain and stiffness are more likely to experience longer duration and long-term sickness absence (source: Bakker et al. - 2003.md, p. 397, 409).

## Measurement

RSI symptoms can be assessed through self-report scales targeting pain, stiffness, and loss of power. In Bakker et al. (2003), a 5-item scale was utilized, with items such as "During the last year, did you experience pain, a stiff feeling, or other discomfort in your arms, wrists, or elbows?" measured on a 5-point frequency scale (1 = never, 5 = always) (source: Bakker et al. - 2003.md, p. 400).
